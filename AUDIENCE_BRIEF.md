# Audience Brief
**Project: Suzhou Dialect — Digital Preservation & the Wicked Problem**
*One-page reference for design, content, and outreach decisions*

---

## Who This Page Is For — and Why They Should Care

The page serves **six distinct audience segments** across two language tracks. They arrive with different questions, different prior knowledge, and different emotional stakes. The design must work for all of them without flattening any of them.

---

## Segment Profiles

### 1. Native Speakers and Local Community 本地人与母语者
**Language track:** ZH primary
**Who:** Suzhou residents (any age) who grew up hearing or speaking the dialect, and their families — including those living in other cities who still associate Suzhounese with home.

**What they already know:** They have felt the loss before they've read a word. They've noticed they don't speak it with their children. They know what Pingtan sounds like.

**What they need from this page:** Validation that what they feel is real and documented — not nostalgia, but a measurable, nameable crisis. And a low-friction way to contribute (the recording portal is specifically for them).

**Why they should care:** This is their language. Losing it is not abstract. When an 80-year-old grandmother is the last person in a family who can say certain phrases, the stakes are not theoretical.

**How to reach them most effectively:**
- WeChat sharing from the Wu Language School (吴语学堂) community
- QR codes at Suzhou cultural sites: Pingjiang Historic District, Suzhou Museum, Kunqu theatre
- Framing: "You are not alone in noticing this" — lead with belonging, not alarm

**Page design notes:** The ZH track's opening pull quote and the Three Generations audio cards are calibrated for this segment. The phrase rotator in the CTA section is for them specifically.

### 2. Chinese Diaspora 海外华人
**Language track:** ZH or EN depending on generation
**Who:** First- and second-generation Chinese living outside mainland China, particularly those with Jiangsu/Zhejiang roots. Includes 1.5-generation who switch between Chinese and English.

**What they already know:** Dialect loss through personal experience. They may no longer speak Suzhounese (or speak it imperfectly), but they remember it as the sound of grandparents, of visits, of a certain quality of intimacy.

**What they need from this page:** The intellectual framework to explain to others why this matters — and to explain to themselves why the guilt or grief they feel has a real basis.

**Why they should care:** Second-generation speakers are among the last who can still recall the dialect as a living thing, not an archive. Sixty percent of Chinese-Canadian second-generation speakers cannot fluently speak their parents' dialect. This page documents what is being lost in their own generation.

**How to reach them most effectively:**
- Diaspora WeChat groups and forums (北美华人, 海外苏州人圈子)
- Bilingual social media posts (content that works in both the ZH and EN cultural registers)
- Frame around identity and intergenerational connection, not linguistic technicality

**Page design notes:** The language toggle is essential for this segment — many will begin in ZH and switch to EN for the academic sections. The demographic dot visualization resonates because it literalizes a family story.

### 3. Linguistics Researchers and Fieldworkers 语言学研究者
**Language track:** EN primary
**Who:** Phonologists, sociolinguists, dialectologists, and language documentation specialists — at any career stage from graduate student to senior professor.

**What they already know:** The Wu Chinese literature. Probably aware of the Linguistic Atlas surveys, possibly familiar with Phonemica. May know the Rittel-Webber framework from adjacent fields.

**What they need from this page:** A single coherent synthesis that maps the full wicked problem structure onto the Suzhounese case — something they can cite, share with students, or use as a launching point for their own research framing.

**Why they should care:** Suzhounese is a living witness to Early Middle Chinese phonology — voiced obstruents, seven tones with register conditioning, complex tone sandhi — that exist nowhere else in a Chinese variety with this speaker base. Every year without systematic documentation is irreversible loss. The wicked problem framing also offers these researchers a theoretical lens for explaining why their work is hard to funders and administrators who expect "solutions."

**How to reach them most effectively:**
- Academic social networks (Academia.edu, ResearchGate)
- Citations in linguistics papers and syllabi
- Sharing in language documentation and endangered language communities (ELDP, ELA networks)
- The page should be citable: include a clear title, date, and author attribution

**Page design notes:** The expandable Wicked Problem table (all 10 properties) and the Phonology Deep-Dive section are specifically for this segment. The tech landscape section gives them an overview they can reference without building it themselves.

### 4. NLP Engineers and Computational Linguists 计算语言学工程师
**Language track:** EN primary
**Who:** Researchers and engineers working on ASR, TTS, speech synthesis, or low-resource NLP — at universities, tech companies, or research labs. Includes people working on Chinese dialect NLP specifically and those exploring transfer learning across tonal languages.

**What they already know:** The data scarcity problem for low-resource languages. Probably know Whisper, FunASR, Qwen3-ASR. May not know the specifics of the Wu tonal system.

**What they need from this page:** A clear technical problem statement: *why* Suzhounese is specifically hard (not just "low-resource" in general), what the bootstrapping paradox means in practice, and what the current corpus landscape looks like so they aren't duplicating work.

**Why they should care:** Suzhounese is a maximally hard case for tonal ASR — 7 tones with phonation conditioning, full voiced obstruent series, and complex sandhi — making it a technically interesting challenge, not just a social good. Solving it advances methods applicable to dozens of other under-resourced tonal languages. And the WenetSpeech-Wu corpus (released 2024) finally provides a foundation to build on.

**How to reach them most effectively:**
- GitHub (link to relevant repos: FunASR, WenetSpeech-Wu)
- arXiv and ACL Anthology sharing
- Interspeech and ICASSP conference communities
- Frame as a hard research problem, not a charitable cause — engineers respond to technically interesting constraints

**Page design notes:** The Technology Landscape status grid and the Bootstrapping Paradox callout box are designed for this segment. The Phonology Deep-Dive (IPA, tone contour diagrams, voiced consonant table) gives them the linguistic grounding without requiring them to dig into primary literature.

### 5. Cultural Heritage Professionals and Policymakers 文化遗产专业人士与政策制定者
**Language track:** EN or ZH depending on institution
**Who:** People working in intangible cultural heritage, UNESCO frameworks, China's Ministry of Culture, provincial cultural bureaus, museum professionals, and language policy researchers.

**What they already know:** The institutional landscape — UNESCO ICH lists, the 2015 government documentation project, general awareness that dialect decline is happening. Probably familiar with Kunqu as a UNESCO case.

**What they need from this page:** The wicked problem framing applied specifically to the policy dimension — why the Putonghua/heritage preservation contradiction is not a simple policy failure but a structural tension that cannot be resolved by more funding or better intentions alone.

**Why they should care:** Kunqu opera is a UNESCO Masterpiece precisely because it depends on Suzhounese phonology. Policy that preserves Kunqu as performance while allowing the dialect to die is preserving the form without the substrate. This is a coherence problem for any cultural heritage institution that takes linguistic grounding seriously.

**How to reach them most effectively:**
- UNESCO ICH networks and newsletters
- China's cultural heritage professional associations
- Suzhou Municipal Culture Bureau channels
- Frame around institutional accountability: "What does it mean to protect a language-dependent art form while the language disappears?"

**Page design notes:** The timeline section (especially the UNESCO 2001 Kunqu milestone) and the Comparative Cases section (policy contrast with Taiwan's Hokkien curriculum mandate) are most relevant for this segment.

---

### 6. Curious General Public 普通读者
**Language track:** Either — often EN for international readers, ZH for domestic Chinese readers outside the Suzhou community
**Who:** Anyone who followed a link — language enthusiasts, China watchers, people interested in endangered languages, students assigned the page, or readers who saw a social media post.

**What they already know:** Probably nothing specific. May have heard of Mandarin vs. dialects in passing. May know of other endangered language situations (Welsh, Gaelic, Navajo).

**What they need from this page:** An emotional entry point (the video), then enough linguistic specificity to understand *why this language in particular* matters, then the intellectual framework that makes the preservation problem genuinely fascinating rather than simply sad.

**Why they should care:** The wicked problem frame is the hook for this segment — it explains why smart, well-meaning people keep failing to solve a problem that seems like it should be solvable. That is a story that generalizes far beyond linguistics. And the 95% → 2% fluency collapse in one generation is the kind of data point that lands even without specialist background.

**How to reach them most effectively:**
- Social media sharing of the video (the 2:30 primer is designed to work as a standalone shareable)
- Coverage in publications covering language, China, or digital humanities (The World of Chinese, Sixth Tone, Atlas Obscura, The Guardian's languages coverage)
- Blog posts and threads quoting the most striking data points
- Frame as a mystery: "Why can't we solve it?" is more compelling than "This is sad."

**Page design notes:** The landing waveform, the background video, and the Three Generations audio cards carry the emotional weight for this segment. The wicked problem paradigm cards (4 cards: complexity, resistance, conflict, shared interests) give them a takeaway they can explain to someone else.

## Why the Bilingual Split Layout Is the Right Call

Most of the page's design choices are legible once you understand that **none of these six segments can be served by a single register**. A page pitched at NLP engineers will lose native speakers. A page pitched at native speakers will lose researchers. The bilingual split — ZH track for emotional/cultural framing, EN track for analytical/technical framing — is not aesthetic novelty. It is the only layout that keeps both the community audience and the research audience in the same document without alienating either.

The language toggle to single-column is the right mobile/focus affordance: it lets each user sink into their register without the other track creating cognitive noise.

---

## What This Page Is Not For

- It is **not** a fundraising page. There are no donation asks. The community portal collects recordings and comments, not money.
- It is **not** a technical documentation site for tools. It explains the technology landscape but does not replace project READMEs.
- It is **not** a news article. It does not pretend to report on recent events; it presents a synthesized analytical argument.

Understanding what the page is *not* for is as important as knowing what it is — it keeps scope creep from diluting the experience for the segments it is actually designed to serve.
